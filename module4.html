Why Evidence Matters in Medical Sales

The difference between a product pitch and a clinical conversation

A doctor's primary obligation is to their patient. Their second obligation is to evidence. When you walk into a clinic and present Epithrive®, the doctor is not thinking about the price or the flavour — they are thinking: "Is there credible evidence that this will benefit my patient?" If you cannot answer that question precisely and confidently, the conversation ends. If you can, it begins.

🎯

For the Surgeon

Show the India study with p-value and wound area reduction. Reference Barbul (Annals of Surgery). Lead with the 72.4% outcome number.

🔬

For the Diabetologist

Lead with Tatti 2012 (DFU healing 247→83 days) and the India study DFU arm. Reinforce with Arginine-NO-microcirculation mechanism.

🏥

For the Intensivist

Lead with Wischmeyer 2003 (45% reduction in ICU infections), Kisil & Gimelfarb 2023 (pressure ulcer healing), and hospital stay reduction data.

Golden rule: Know which studies are most relevant to each specialist. A surgeon responds to collagen deposition data. A diabetologist responds to DFU healing time data. An intensivist responds to infection reduction and hospital stay data. Same evidence library — calibrated delivery.

The Evidence Pyramid

Understanding study types — so you can explain why the evidence is credible
Meta-Analysis / Systematic Review

Pools multiple studies. Highest statistical power. Arribas-López et al. (2021) — pooled 44 human studies on Arginine & Glutamine wound healing. Our formulation class is supported here.

Randomised Controlled Trial (RCT)

Gold standard. Random allocation eliminates selection bias. Williams & Barbul (2002, Annals of Surgery), Armstrong et al. (2014), Cereda et al. — all RCTs supporting the formulation class.

Prospective Comparative Study

The Epithrive® India study (CSR-ETH-2025-001) sits here — prospective, comparative, pre-registered protocol, Indian patient population, 60 subjects, p=0.004. This is strong and clinically relevant evidence.

Retrospective / Case Series

Sipahi et al. (2013), Kisil & Gimelfarb (2023), Epithrive® case studies. Real-world supporting evidence. Not primary — but strengthens the clinical picture alongside stronger designs.

Your message to doctors: "The evidence base for Epithrive® operates at multiple levels — a meta-analysis of 44 human studies, multiple RCTs on the formulation class, and our own Indian prospective comparative study with 60 patients showing p=0.004. This is not one isolated finding — it is a consistent signal across study designs and geographies."

What These Numbers Actually Mean

The 72.4% figure means that on average, patients in the Epithrive® arm saw their wound area shrink to 27.6% of its original size over 8 weeks. That is near-complete closure for many patients — not just improvement.

The 20.3 percentage point gap is the absolute difference in outcomes between the two arms — where the only variable was Epithrive®. Both groups received the same standard wound care. The nutrition was the sole differentiator.

The 39% relative improvement means the Epithrive® arm healed 39% better than the standard care arm relative to what standard care achieved. Not 39 percentage points — 39% relatively better.

p = 0.004 means there is a 0.4% probability this result occurred by chance. The statistical threshold for significance is 5% (p < 0.05). At 0.4%, this finding is far stronger than the minimum required for clinical significance.

Important context for doctors: Both arms in this study received identical standard wound care — debridement, dressings, infection control, glycaemic management as applicable. Epithrive® was added as an adjunct, not a replacement. The observed difference is attributable solely to the nutritional intervention. This is the most important point to make when presenting the study.

Reading Clinical Data

Six terms you must understand cold — and be able to explain simply to a doctor

Statistical Literacy for MRs

p-value
The probability that the result occurred by chance. Lower = stronger result.
Our study: p = 0.004 · Threshold for significance: p < 0.05 ✓
Absolute Difference
The raw percentage-point gap between the two groups' outcomes.
72.4% − 52.1% = +20.3 percentage points
Relative Improvement
How much better the treatment arm performed relative to the control arm's outcome.
20.3 ÷ 52.1 × 100 = +39% relative improvement
Open-Label Study
Both patients and investigators know who receives the intervention. Prospective = patients enrolled before outcomes are known.
Our study design — appropriate for this type of nutritional intervention research
n (Sample Size)
Number of participants. Larger n = more statistical power to detect real differences.
Our study: n = 60 (30 per arm) — appropriately powered for pilot evidence
Adjunct Intervention
Added to existing standard of care — not replacing it. The critical framing for Epithrive®.
Epithrive® is always adjunct, never primary. Both arms had identical SOC.
Simple explanation for a busy doctor: "p = 0.004 means if you ran this study 1000 times, only 4 times would the Epithrive® arm show better results by chance alone. In other words, we are 99.6% confident the difference is real, not random. That's a strong finding."

Compliant Evidence Communication

The traffic-light guide — what you can say, what needs framing, and what is never permitted

✓ Green — Say Freely

  • "In the clinical study, the Epithrive® arm showed 72.4% wound area reduction at Week 8 vs. 52.1% in standard care (p=0.004)"
  • "The formulation class is supported by a 2021 meta-analysis of 44 human studies"
  • "Williams & Barbul (Annals of Surgery 2002) showed collagen deposition significantly enhanced with Arg+HMB+Gln"
  • "Supports wound healing as an adjunct to standard care"
  • "Contains clinically researched doses of Arginine, Glutamine, HMB, and Marine Collagen Peptides"
  • "Nutritional support for tissue repair"

△ Amber — Use With Framing

  • "2× faster healing" — must attribute: "Cereda et al. found approximately 2× faster pressure ulcer healing with arginine-enriched formula"
  • "Healing in 15 days" — must be framed as: "In this documented case, a patient experienced near-complete closure in 15 days"
  • "The Armstrong RCT" — acknowledge the primary outcome limitation; lead with subgroup finding for malnourished patients
  • "Hospital stay reduction" — say "studies have shown" + cite Williams 2002; don't present as a guaranteed outcome
  • Any quantified outcome claim must be attributed to a specific study, not stated as a product guarantee

✗ Red — Never Say

  • "Epithrive® heals / treats / cures wounds"
  • "Guaranteed to close the wound in X weeks"
  • "FSMP" or "Food for Special Medical Purposes" — not approved yet
  • "This will definitely reduce hospital stay by 3–4 days"
  • "Works better than antibiotics / dressings"
  • "Replaces standard wound care"
  • Any disease treatment claim without CDSCO drug approval

The Compliant vs. Non-Compliant Comparison

✗ Non-Compliant
"Epithrive® heals diabetic wounds 39% faster."
"This product treats pressure sores and bedsores."
✓ Compliant
"In our clinical study (CSR-ETH-2025-001), patients in the Epithrive® arm showed 72.4% wound area reduction vs. 52.1% with standard care alone at Week 8 — p=0.004."
"Epithrive® supports wound healing as a nutritional adjunct. It provides the specific substrates the body needs for tissue repair alongside standard wound care."
Mandatory disclaimer — always present on any printed material you leave behind:

"Dietary supplement. Not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease. For nutritional support as an adjunct to standard wound care."

Using Evidence in Doctor Calls

How to introduce, present, and land clinical data in a real conversation

Script A — Introducing the India Study to a Surgeon (First Call)

MR Doctor, I wanted to share a clinical finding from an Indian prospective study we recently completed — 60 patients across DFU, pressure injuries, and post-surgical wounds. It's particularly relevant because it was conducted in our population.
DR Go ahead.
MR Both groups received identical standard wound care. The only difference was the addition of one sachet of Epithrive® per day. At Week 8, the Epithrive® arm showed 72.4% mean wound area reduction vs. 52.1% in the standard care arm — p=0.004. That's a 20-point absolute difference, statistically significant, and reflects a 39% relative improvement in healing rate.
DR That's a meaningful difference. Who was the PI?
MR Dr. B. Praful, MS Ortho — Orthopaedic Surgeon — as principal investigator. Co-investigator was Dr. K Aruna, MD IDCCM — Critical Care. The full CSR is available if you'd like to review it, or I can leave the A4 summary today.
Notice: the MR presents the finding precisely, attributes it correctly, names the investigators, and offers the leave-behind naturally — not as a pitch, as a service.

Script B — Responding to "Show Me the Global Evidence" (Diabetologist)

DR Your India study is interesting but n=60 is small. Is there larger global evidence?
MR Absolutely — and that's a fair question. The global evidence base for this formulation class is substantial. For DFU specifically, Tatti et al. published data showing the Arg+Gln+HMB formula reduced diabetic foot ulcer healing time from 247 days to 83 days in longstanding neuropathic ulcers. For collagen deposition, Williams and Barbul at Johns Hopkins — published in Annals of Surgery 2002 — showed a double-blind RCT where the supplement significantly increased wound collagen in elderly patients.
MR There's also a 2021 systematic review and meta-analysis in Nutrients journal — 44 human studies — that confirmed significant effects of Arginine and Glutamine supplementation on multiple wound healing markers including nitrogen balance, cytokine levels, and length of hospital stay. Epithrive® delivers the same active components at therapeutic doses in a single sachet.
Key technique: bridge from the India study limitation to the global evidence without being defensive. "Fair question + here's the larger picture" is always more credible than defensiveness.

Script C — Using Case Study Evidence (ICU Physician)

DR I have a bedridden patient with a Stage III pressure ulcer — albumin is 2.9. What have you actually seen this product do in someone like that?
MR We have documented a case with very similar parameters — a diabetic patient with a chronic wound and nutritional compromise. After starting Epithrive® one sachet daily alongside ongoing standard care, the wound showed organised granulation tissue by Day 12, wound margin contraction and reduced surface area by Day 25, and complete closure by Day 35. The photographic documentation is available.
MR For pressure ulcers specifically in older patients, Kisil and Gimelfarb published a 2023 case-control study showing HMB+Arg+Gln reduced median time to pressure ulcer healing from 218 days to 170 days in elderly patients with multiple comorbidities — including diabetes and dementia. That's the population profile you're describing.
ICU physicians respond to patient-like-mine evidence. Lead with the case that mirrors their patient, then support with the published data that confirms the pattern.

Handling Evidence Objections

Every clinical data challenge — and how to answer it with confidence
Doctor's Objection What They Really Mean The Right Response
"n=60 is too small to mean anything." Wants larger evidence. May have RCT standards. Acknowledge the point, then pivot: "You're right that it's a pilot-scale study — and that's why we also have the global evidence base. Williams & Barbul's RCT in Annals of Surgery with elderly volunteers, Cereda's RCT showing 2× faster pressure ulcer healing, and a 2021 meta-analysis of 44 human studies — all consistently point in the same direction. The India study adds Indian population relevance to an already-established formulation class."
"The Armstrong 2014 RCT was negative — same formulation class, no difference in wound closure." Has read the literature. Impressed — treat seriously. "The Armstrong study is important to understand correctly. The primary outcome — wound closure in the overall population — showed no significant difference. But the subgroup analysis showed clear benefit in high-risk patients — specifically those with albumin ≤17 mg/dL pre-albumin and poor limb perfusion. That's exactly the malnourished patient we're targeting with Epithrive®. The lesson from Armstrong is that patient selection matters: whole-population effects are diluted when you include patients who don't have nutritional deficits."
"Open-label studies are biased — both patient and investigator know who gets the supplement." Knows study design limitations. "That's a fair methodological point. The primary outcome — wound area measured photographically — is objective, which reduces assessment bias considerably. The study was prospective with a pre-registered protocol. For nutritional interventions, open-label designs are standard — blinding a nutritional supplement against placebo is operationally difficult. The global RCT evidence supports the same conclusion with blinded designs, so the India study result is consistent with stronger methodology."
"I need a peer-reviewed published RCT from India before I recommend it." High evidentiary bar. Wants publication. "That's a completely fair standard, and it's one we're working toward. The clinical study report is available now for your review. In the meantime, the global published RCT evidence — Williams & Barbul 2002 (Annals of Surgery), Cereda 2015, and the 2021 meta-analysis of 44 human studies — provides published, peer-reviewed support for the formulation class. Would you be willing to try Epithrive® on one or two carefully selected malnourished wound patients while we progress toward publication?"
"72.4% vs 52.1% — is that really meaningful? Both groups healed." Questioning clinical significance, not just statistical. "Both groups did improve — that's expected, because both received good standard wound care. The question is whether adding Epithrive® made a meaningful additional difference. A 20-percentage-point absolute difference with p=0.004 is statistically significant and clinically meaningful. To put it concretely: in a patient whose wound was 10 cm² — the Epithrive® arm reached approximately 2.8 cm² remaining vs. 4.8 cm² in the control arm. That is the difference between near-closure and still-open, which translates directly to fewer dressing changes, lower infection risk, and shorter recovery."
"The case studies don't count as evidence." Correct — and showing they understand evidence grades. "You're absolutely right — case studies are not primary evidence. They sit at the base of the evidence pyramid. I'm not presenting them as proof of efficacy — I'm using them to illustrate what the published evidence looks like in a real patient. The primary evidence is the clinical study (CSR-ETH-2025-001) and the global literature. The case documentation shows the biological mechanism in action: granulation at Day 10, contraction at Day 25, closure at Day 35 — consistent with the formulation's mechanism."
"This is just a nutraceutical — it doesn't have drug approval." May want pharmaceutical-grade evidence standards. "Correct — Epithrive® is a nutraceutical, registered under FSSAI. This is also why it doesn't require a prescription, so your patients can access it immediately once you recommend it. For nutraceuticals, the evidence standard is clinical study data and mechanistic rationale — which Epithrive® has in the form of the India study and the global formulation-class evidence. The ingredients themselves are well-established nutritional substrates with deep mechanistic and clinical research. This is not an untested supplement — it is a clinical nutrition formulation."
"I've never heard of the investigators." Wants validation of the research team's credentials. "Dr. B. Praful is an Orthopaedic Surgeon with extensive wound case experience — his specialty means he manages the exact patient population the study enrolled. Dr. K Aruna, MD IDCCM, specialises in critical care and internal medicine — the other major wound patient source. Dr. Prathmesh, the Nutrition Consultant, brings clinical dietetics validation. The study was conducted with IRB-equivalent ethics oversight. The full CSR is available if you'd like to review the methods and findings directly."
"Why would I trust this over established brands like Ensure or Fresubin that have decades of evidence?" Comparing to general ONS with larger evidence bases. "Ensure and Fresubin are excellent products for general nutritional support — and they have the evidence base you'd expect for that use case. But they were not designed for wound healing. They don't contain therapeutic doses of Arginine (Epithrive® has 7g — most ONS have under 1g). They don't contain HMB or Marine Collagen Peptides at all. The formulation class that Epithrive® belongs to — Arg+Gln+HMB+Collagen — has its own evidence base in wound-specific settings. These are different products designed for different clinical purposes. Using Ensure for wound healing is like using a general multivitamin for chemotherapy-related deficiency — it's not wrong, but it's not targeted."
The meta-skill: Every evidence objection is an opportunity to demonstrate clinical credibility. Doctors who challenge the evidence are engaged — they're thinking. Doctors who accept without questioning are not really listening. Respond to challenges with specific data, not defensiveness. Acknowledge limitations honestly, then reframe with the broader picture. A doctor who sees you handle a tough question well will trust you more than one who sees you deflect it.
