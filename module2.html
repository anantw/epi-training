The T.I.M.E. Framework

The global standard for structured wound bed preparation — what every wound care team follows

T.I.M.E. is the internationally recognised wound bed preparation framework developed by the European Wound Management Association (EWMA). It provides a systematic approach to wound management by addressing four core barriers to healing. Every wound care specialist uses this mental model — knowing it puts you on equal footing in any clinical conversation.

T

Tissue Management

Non-viable tissue removal
  • Remove necrotic, sloughy, or fibrinous tissue from wound bed
  • Prevents bacterial growth and chronic inflammation
  • Creates a clean base for granulation tissue to form
  • Methods: surgical debridement, enzymatic agents, autolytic dressings
  • Healthy wound bed should be: pink/red, moist, granular
Epithrive® role: Arginine → collagen scaffold to replace removed tissue
I

Infection / Inflammation

Control the biological burden
  • Identify and control bacterial bioburden — from contamination → colonisation → infection
  • Critically colonised wounds: high bacterial load without classic signs
  • Local infection: erythema, warmth, pain, purulent exudate
  • Chronic inflammation: persistent elevated MMPs → ECM destruction
  • Treatment: topical antimicrobials, systemic antibiotics if spreading
Epithrive® role: Glutamine → immune cell fuel → infection control support
M

Moisture Balance

Optimal wound environment
  • Wound healing requires a moist (not wet, not dry) environment
  • Too dry: cell migration is impaired; eschar forms over surface
  • Too wet (maceration): surrounding skin breaks down; wound expands
  • Exudate management is the primary purpose of modern dressings
  • Correct dressing absorbs excess exudate without drying the wound bed
Epithrive® role: Collagen peptides → ECM integrity → improved exudate control
E

Epithelial / Edge Advancement

Stimulate wound closure
  • Keratinocytes must migrate from wound edges across the wound bed to close the surface
  • Non-advancing edges signal a stalled wound — cells are senescent or hypoxic
  • Wound contraction (myofibroblasts) pulls edges together simultaneously
  • Undermining or rolling edges indicate chronic wound failure
  • Stimulation methods: debridement, growth factors, surgical techniques
Epithrive® role: Vitamin A + Zinc → keratinocyte migration; Arginine → angiogenesis at edges
How to use T.I.M.E. in a doctor conversation: "I know you're managing this wound using the T.I.M.E. framework. You're addressing the tissue, the infection, and the moisture balance with local care. What Epithrive® adds is the systemic nutritional substrate that supports E — epithelial advancement — and everything that drives it: angiogenesis, collagen deposition, immune competence, and anti-catabolism."

Wound Assessment

What the wound care team evaluates at every visit — know the parameters
📐

Size & Dimensions

Length × Width (cm²) measured photographically or with ruler. Wound area reduction percentage is the key healing metric — exactly what the India clinical study used. Depth measured with sterile probe.

Clinical study basis: 72.4% wound area reduction at Week 8
🔬

Tissue Type

Necrotic (black/brown, eschar) · Slough (yellow/white, fibrinous) · Granulation (red/pink, healthy) · Epithelial (pink, advancing) · Hypergranulation (raised above surface — excess). Progress = movement from necrotic toward epithelial.

Red granulation = wound progressing. Yellow slough = stalled.
💧

Exudate

Amount: none → light → moderate → heavy. Type: serous (clear, normal), haemoserous (pink), purulent (green/yellow, infected). Excessive exudate with odour → infection or high MMP activity → wound stalling.

Decreasing exudate + improving clarity = healing progress
🔴

Wound Edges & Margins

Attached vs. undermined (tunnelling underneath). Rolled/epiboly edges = non-advancing; chronic wound. Well-defined, attached, advancing edges = healing. Undermining depth and direction documented in clock-face notation.

Advancing edges = Epithrive® doing its job systemically
🌡️

Peri-Wound Skin

Maceration (white, soggy — too wet). Erythema (redness spreading beyond wound margin — infection). Induration (firm, raised — spreading infection). Callus formation (pressure wounds). Skin temperature differential vs. normal tissue.

Vitamin E + Selenium → peri-wound oxidative protection
⏱️

Healing Trajectory

Is the wound improving, static, or deteriorating? Rule of thumb: if wound area has not reduced ≥30% in 4 weeks with standard care, the healing trajectory is insufficient — nutritional support should be considered. Stalled = SNAP Rule trigger.

No 30% reduction in 4 weeks → Epithrive® conversation
The MR's assessment literacy goal: You don't need to assess wounds yourself — that is the clinician's role. You need to understand the terms, so when a doctor says "wound has slough but no granulation" or "edges are undermined and not advancing," you understand what this means clinically — and can recognise it as the exact scenario where nutritional substrate support is most needed.

Debridement — Types & Rationale

The cornerstone of T–Tissue management — know all four methods

Debridement is the removal of non-viable, infected, or foreign tissue from a wound to expose healthy tissue beneath and allow healing to progress. It is the most fundamental wound care intervention. Understanding debridement makes you credible with surgeons and wound nurses — and creates the critical context for why nutritional support matters: debridement dramatically increases metabolic demand for tissue repair.

Surgical / Sharp

Fastest. Most complete.

Scalpel, scissors, or curette used by surgeon or trained clinician. Removes all necrotic tissue in a single session. Creates a bleeding wound bed — signals the body to restart healing cascade. Gold standard for heavily infected or heavily necrotic wounds.

When used: Wagner 3–4 DFU, heavily infected pressure ulcers, post-traumatic wounds with necrotic tissue, large burns.

MR context: Surgical debridement massively ↑ metabolic demand → substrate need ↑ immediately
Enzymatic

Selective. Ongoing.

Topical enzyme preparations (collagenase-based, e.g., Iruxol Mono, Collatamp) break down necrotic tissue selectively without damaging healthy tissue. Applied under dressing, changed every 1–2 days. Slower than surgical but more selective and patient-friendly.

When used: Moderate necrosis, outpatient settings, patients who cannot tolerate surgical debridement (elderly, anticoagulated).

MR context: Enzymatic agents work on local environment; Epithrive® supports systemic substrate
Autolytic

Gentlest. Self-directed.

Uses the body's own enzymes and moisture — created by an occlusive or semi-occlusive dressing (hydrocolloid, hydrogel) — to soften and liquefy necrotic tissue. Painless, patient-friendly, no specialist skill required. Slowest method. Not suitable for infected wounds.

When used: Light to moderate slough, community settings, elderly patients, palliative wound management.

MR context: Autolytic takes weeks — Epithrive® shortens the systemic healing lag
Mechanical / Biological

Targeted. Emerging.

Mechanical: Wet-to-dry gauze (non-selective, painful, now largely abandoned), wound irrigation with saline or PHMB, ultrasound-assisted debridement. Biological (Maggot therapy): sterile Lucilia sericata larvae consume necrotic tissue selectively — highly effective in deep, refractory wounds. Used in specialised centres.

When used: NPWT (Negative Pressure Wound Therapy) also removes slough continuously.

MR context: Whatever method — substrate supply from Epithrive® remains the limiting factor
The critical nutritional link: Every debridement method, whether surgical or enzymatic, removes not just dead tissue but also growth factors and wound signals — resetting the wound to an earlier healing stage. This dramatically increases the demand for Arginine (NO → revascularisation), Glutamine (immune reinitiation), and Collagen Peptides (ECM scaffold rebuilding). Epithrive® initiated within 24–48 hours of surgical debridement provides the substrates the wound biology needs to capitalise on the cleaned wound bed.

Recognising Wound Infection

The progression from contamination to systemic infection — and why nutrition matters at every stage

⚠️ Local Infection

  • Increased pain, warmth, erythema
  • Purulent exudate (yellow/green)
  • Wound odour
  • Delayed healing despite good care
  • Friable granulation tissue that bleeds easily
  • Treatment: topical antimicrobials (silver/PHMB/honey)

🚨 Spreading Infection (Cellulitis)

  • Erythema extending >2cm beyond wound margin
  • Lymphangitis (red streaks)
  • Lymphadenopathy
  • Systemic signs beginning (fever, raised WBC)
  • Treatment: oral / IV antibiotics + local care
  • Key risk: diabetic patients may have no fever (neuropathy)

🆘 Systemic Infection (Sepsis)

  • Fever >38°C or hypothermia <36°C
  • HR >90, RR >20, altered consciousness
  • Elevated WBC, CRP, procalcitonin
  • Requires IV antibiotics, hospital admission
  • Septic patients are in extreme catabolic state — highest substrate need
  • Glutamine depletion is most severe here
The nutrition-infection connection for MRs: Glutamine is the primary fuel for immune cells (neutrophils and macrophages) — the cells that fight wound infection. In a malnourished patient with a chronic wound, Glutamine is often depleted 50–80% below normal. This means the immune system is fighting with an empty tank. Epithrive® (7g Glutamine per sachet) refuels this immune response — which is why patients on Epithrive® show faster resolution of wound colonisation alongside appropriate antimicrobial therapy.

Specialised Care by Wound Type

Beyond dressings — the condition-specific interventions your doctors perform

🦶 DFU — Offloading

Critical intervention alongside nutrition and dressings

  • Total Contact Cast (TCC): Gold standard. Distributes plantar pressure evenly across the foot. Forces compliance. Significantly reduces DFU healing time.
  • Instant TCC (iTCC) / Removable Cast Walker locked: Effective when TCC is not feasible. Removable but locked to ensure compliance.
  • Forefoot offloading shoe / half shoe: Removes pressure from forefoot wounds. Less effective than TCC.
  • Custom insoles and therapeutic footwear: Prevents recurrence post-healing — critical given 70% recurrence rate in DFU.
  • Wheelchair or crutches: Non-weight-bearing for severe cases.
Epithrive® + offloading = systemic + mechanical healing. One without the other is suboptimal.

🦵 VLU — Compression Therapy

The primary driver of venous ulcer healing

  • Multi-layer compression bandaging: 4-layer (e.g., Profore) creates sustained 40mmHg at ankle — reverses venous hypertension. Gold standard. Changed weekly.
  • Short-stretch (inelastic) bandaging: High working pressure with walking. Used in ambulant patients.
  • Compression hosiery (stockings): Maintenance/prevention after healing. Class II (23–32mmHg) or III (34–46mmHg).
  • Intermittent Pneumatic Compression (IPC): Machine-driven compression for non-ambulant or lymphoedema patients.
  • Rule: Compression is only safe when ABPI >0.8 (adequate arterial flow confirmed by Doppler).
Compression reverses venous hypertension locally. Epithrive® supports the angiogenesis and collagen synthesis that compression creates the opportunity for.

🛏️ Pressure Injuries — Repositioning

Prevention is always better than treatment

  • 2-hourly repositioning: The fundamental intervention. A nurse or caregiver manually turns the patient to prevent sustained pressure on any one point.
  • Pressure-relieving surfaces: Alternating pressure mattresses (dynamic), high-specification foam (static). Reduces interface pressure below capillary closing pressure (~32mmHg).
  • Positioning devices: Heel boots (remove heel from mattress entirely), positioning wedges (30° lateral tilt), foam props.
  • Skin care: Moisture barriers to protect skin from incontinence-related moisture damage (IAD). Skin assessment at every reposition.
  • Friction reduction: Slide sheets, hoists — reduce skin shear during repositioning.
HMB from Epithrive® preserves the lean mass that protects bony prominences — a malnourished patient has less padding and higher pressure injury risk.

💉 Diabetes Management in DFU

Glycaemic control is foundational to wound healing

  • HbA1c target: <7.5% for active wound healing. High glucose impairs neutrophil function, reduces endothelial NO production, and glycates collagen (making it dysfunctional).
  • Blood glucose monitoring: Daily or per ward protocol. Hyperglycaemia (>200mg/dL) significantly impairs all wound healing phases.
  • Insulin optimisation: In hospitalised DFU patients, IV insulin infusion often used to tightly control glucose perioperatively and during debridement periods.
  • Foot care education: Daily inspection, appropriate footwear, early reporting of breaks in skin — preventing the DFU cycle from restarting.
  • Vascular assessment: Ankle-Brachial Pressure Index (ABPI) — identifies ischaemia, guides revascularisation decisions.
Epithrive® has no added sugar — safe alongside insulin therapy. Arginine supports the NO production that diabetes impairs at the endothelial level.

Nutrition in Global Wound Care Guidelines

What ESPEN, EPUAP, and NPUAP formally recommend — the evidence base behind Epithrive®'s positioning

When a doctor asks "is wound nutrition an established practice?" the answer is yes — and it is backed by the three most authoritative bodies in wound care. These guidelines form the clinical rationale for Epithrive® as a medically grounded nutritional adjunct, not a novel or unproven intervention.

ESPEN

European Society for Clinical Nutrition & Metabolism

  • Recommends oral nutritional supplementation (ONS) for patients with pressure injuries or at high nutritional risk
  • Protein requirements increase in wound healing: 1.2–2.0g protein/kg/day for wound patients
  • Arginine supplementation formally recommended in pressure ulcer patients who are malnourished or at nutritional risk
  • Recommends micronutrient supplementation (Zinc, Vitamin C) in deficient wound patients
Epithrive® directly delivers ESPEN-recommended substrates: Arg, protein-equivalent amino acids, Zinc, Vitamin C — in a single daily sachet
EPUAP / NPIAP

European / National Pressure Injury Advisory Panel

  • Strong recommendation to offer high-protein, high-calorie ONS to people with pressure injuries who cannot meet nutritional requirements through diet alone
  • Recommends Arginine, Zinc, and Vitamin C supplementation specifically for pressure injury healing
  • Nutrition screening (MUST or equivalent) recommended for every pressure injury patient — reflects importance of nutritional status
  • States: "Malnutrition is a major risk factor for pressure injury development and delayed healing"
EPUAP explicitly recommends Arg + Zinc + Vit C combination — exactly what Epithrive® provides alongside Glutamine, HMB, and Collagen
NPUAP / IWGDF

International Working Group on the Diabetic Foot

  • Nutritional support formally recognised as an adjunct to DFU management
  • IWGDF guidelines identify malnutrition as an independent risk factor for poor DFU outcomes and amputation
  • Protein and micronutrient supplementation recommended for malnourished DFU patients
  • Nutritional assessment (albumin, pre-albumin, MUST) recommended as part of DFU workup
  • Arginine's role in NO-mediated microcirculation improvement cited in evidence-based guidelines
IWGDF identifies malnutrition as amputation risk — Epithrive® addresses this with the specific substrates that DFU healing requires
How to use this with doctors: "Wound nutrition isn't a novel concept — ESPEN, EPUAP, and the IWGDF all formally recommend nutritional supplementation for wound patients.
