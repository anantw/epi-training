What Is a Wound?

Definition, acute vs. chronic, and why the distinction matters
Clinical definition: A wound is the disruption in the physical continuity of functional tissue — skin, muscle, blood vessels, or underlying structures — caused by injury, disease, surgery, or pressure.

The human body has a remarkable built-in repair system. When tissue is damaged, a cascade of highly coordinated biological events begins within seconds — summoning cells, building new vessels, manufacturing collagen, and closing the gap. In healthy individuals with adequate nutrition, this system works flawlessly. In your patients — diabetics, the elderly, the malnourished — it breaks down. Understanding why is your competitive advantage.

✓ Acute Wound — Normal Healing

  • Cause: Surgical incision, laceration, trauma
  • Orderly progression through all 4 phases
  • Closes within days to weeks
  • Adequate blood flow, normal immune response
  • Adequate nutritional reserves
  • Examples: Clean surgical cut, minor laceration

✗ Chronic Wound — Stalled Healing

  • Duration: No significant progress in 2+ weeks
  • Stuck in the inflammatory phase — cannot progress
  • Excessive MMP activity destroys ECM as fast as it forms
  • Impaired blood flow — hypoxic wound bed
  • Nutritional depletion — substrates for repair are exhausted
  • Examples: DFU, pressure ulcer, venous leg ulcer
The key insight for every MR: A chronic wound is not just a wound that hasn't healed yet. It is a wound where the biological repair machinery has failed due to one or more systemic deficits — most commonly nutritional depletion, impaired circulation, or persistent infection. Local wound dressing manages the environment. Systemic nutrition restores the machinery.

The 4 Phases of Wound Healing

Sequential, overlapping, substrate-hungry — and reversible in chronic wounds

Phase 1

Hemostasis

Minutes → Hours
  • Blood vessel constriction (vasoconstriction) limits bleeding
  • Platelets aggregate at wound site — activated by exposed collagen
  • Fibrin clot forms — temporary scaffold and physical barrier
  • Platelets release growth factors (PDGF, TGF-β) — signal to immune cells
  • Complement cascade activated
🔴 The clot is not just a plug — it is the alarm signal that launches everything that follows.
Phase 2

Inflammation

Day 0 → Day 5
  • Vasodilation — wound becomes red, warm, swollen (classic signs)
  • Neutrophils arrive first (within hours) — kill bacteria, clear debris
  • Macrophages arrive Day 2–3 — engulf debris, release cytokines and growth factors
  • Cytokines (IL-1, TNF-α, IL-6) recruit fibroblasts and endothelial cells
  • Arginine → Nitric Oxide → vasodilation → better cell delivery
🟠 This phase MUST resolve. Persistent inflammation = chronic wound. Redness that never decreases is a red flag.
Phase 3

Proliferation

Day 3 → Day 21
  • Angiogenesis: new blood vessels grow into the wound bed
  • Fibroblasts migrate in and start producing collagen III (initial scaffold)
  • Granulation tissue forms — the pink/red healthy tissue you want to see
  • Re-epithelialization: keratinocytes migrate across the wound surface
  • Wound contraction begins — edges pull together (myofibroblasts)
🟢 Healthy granulation tissue is bright red/pink, moist, and granular. Pale/yellow tissue = problem.
Phase 4

Remodeling

Day 21 → 2 Years
  • Collagen III replaced by stronger, organised Collagen I
  • Matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) regulate ECM breakdown/rebuilding
  • Cross-linking of collagen fibres (via Copper + lysyl oxidase)
  • Tensile strength builds to 80–85% of normal tissue
  • Excess vessels regress; scar matures and flattens
🔵 Scar is permanent but tensile strength improves for months. HMB preserves muscle mass throughout this phase.
Critical teaching point: These phases are sequential but overlapping — they don't have clean start/end dates. A wound can be in Phase 2 and Phase 3 simultaneously in different zones. More importantly, in chronic wounds, they are not linear — the wound can regress from Phase 3 back to Phase 2 if infection occurs or nutrition deteriorates. Healing is not a one-way door.

Key Cells in Wound Healing

Six cell types — what they do, when they arrive, and what happens when they fail

Wound healing is a highly choreographed cellular performance. Each cell type has a precise role and arrives at a specific time. When nutrition is depleted, these cells lose their fuel and the performance degrades. As an MR, knowing these cells by name and function elevates every clinical conversation.

🩸

Platelets

First Responders · Signal Senders

Activated by exposed collagen when injury occurs. Aggregate to form the initial clot, stop bleeding, and release a surge of growth factors (PDGF, TGF-β, VEGF) that recruit every other cell in the cascade. The platelet is the starting gun of wound repair.

Arrive: Seconds to Minutes
🛡️

Neutrophils

Bacteria Killers · Debris Clearers

First immune cells to arrive. Phagocytose (engulf and destroy) bacteria and cellular debris. Release proteases and ROS (reactive oxygen species) to sterilise the wound. Glutamine is the primary fuel for neutrophil activity — depletion directly compromises early infection control.

Arrive: Hours 2–48
🧹

Macrophages

Orchestrators · Phase Transition Managers

Arrive after neutrophils, continue cleanup, and critically, release the cytokines and growth factors that transition the wound from inflammation to proliferation. Without functional macrophages, the wound cannot progress past Phase 2. Glutamine is their primary fuel. Impaired macrophage function = stalled wound.

Arrive: Day 2–5
🏗️

Fibroblasts

Collagen Factories · Granulation Builders

The primary collagen-producing cells. Migrate into the wound bed and begin synthesising Collagen III to form the initial scaffold (granulation tissue). Later produce Collagen I for tensile strength. Fuelled by Glutamine; require Arginine (→ Proline → Collagen), Vitamin C (hydroxylation), Copper (cross-linking), and Zinc (proliferation).

Active: Day 3–21+
🧬

Keratinocytes

Skin Closers · Surface Restorers

Epithelial cells that migrate across the wound surface from wound edges (and hair follicle remnants). They "close the roof" of the wound through re-epithelialization. Vitamin A drives keratinocyte differentiation; Zinc supports their proliferation. Without adequate Vitamin A and Zinc, re-epithelialization is delayed and the wound surface stays open.

Active: Day 4–21
🩹

Endothelial Cells

New Vessel Builders · Angiogenesis Drivers

Form the walls of new blood vessels growing into the wound (angiogenesis). Without new vessel formation, the wound bed cannot receive oxygen, nutrients, or immune cells. Arginine → Nitric Oxide drives endothelial activation and new vessel growth. Impaired NO production in diabetics directly explains why DFU wounds are hypoxic and slow to heal.

Active: Day 5–14
Pitch point: When you tell a doctor that Epithrive® provides 7g L-Glutamine — you can now explain that this directly fuels neutrophils and macrophages (infection control) and fibroblasts (collagen production). That's not a marketing claim. That's cell biology. Doctors respond to that.

What Makes Wounds Stall

The biology of failure — and where nutrition fits in

🔄 The Vicious Cycle: Persistent infection → elevated MMPs → ECM destroyed → growth factors destroyed → angiogenesis fails → hypoxia → more necrosis → more infection → loop continues
1

MMP Overexpression

Matrix metalloproteinases — the enzymes that remodel ECM — become chronically overactive in stalled wounds. They degrade collagen, growth factors, and new granulation tissue faster than it can be laid down. The wound is simultaneously building and being destroyed. Zinc regulates MMP activity; zinc deficiency amplifies this problem.

2

Impaired Angiogenesis

The wound bed cannot recruit new blood vessels because Nitric Oxide production is impaired (especially in diabetics — reduced eNOS activity). Without new vessels, the wound is hypoxic. Hypoxia impairs fibroblast activity, collagen synthesis, and immune cell function. A hypoxic wound is a stalled wound. Arginine → NO reverses this.

3

Fibroblast Senescence

In chronic wound environments, fibroblasts lose their ability to divide and produce collagen — they become "old" and unresponsive. This is driven by oxidative stress, elevated TGF-β, and nutritional deficiency. Senescent fibroblasts cannot build granulation tissue even if the substrates are supplied — which is why early nutritional intervention matters more than late intervention.

4

Bacterial Biofilm

Chronic wounds frequently harbour bacterial biofilm — a protective polysaccharide matrix that shields bacteria from antibiotics and the immune system. Biofilm bacteria release collagen-degrading enzymes and produce toxins that destroy growth factors. This explains why antibiotics alone often fail and why immune competence (Glutamine) is essential alongside local care.

5

Nutritional Substrate Depletion

The single most correctable cause of wound stalling. Wound healing is metabolically demanding — consuming Arginine (for NO and collagen), Glutamine (for immune cells), Zinc, Vitamin C, and Copper at rates far exceeding normal dietary intake. When these substrates are exhausted, the biological machinery simply stops. This is the exact gap Epithrive® fills.

6

Catabolism & Muscle Wasting

Chronic illness triggers a catabolic state where the body breaks down muscle protein to fuel the inflammatory response and immune system. Patients lose lean mass, become immobile, and are at risk of developing new pressure injuries. Immobility impairs tissue perfusion and further slows wound healing. Ca-HMB specifically interrupts this catabolic cycle, preserving the muscle mass required for recovery.

Diabetic Foot Ulcer (DFU)

Wagner Classification System

The most critical wound type in India. DFU results from the combination of peripheral neuropathy (loss of protective sensation — patient can't feel injury) and peripheral vascular disease (impaired blood flow). The diabetic environment also impairs endothelial NO production, macrophage function, and collagen synthesis — creating a triple deficit.

Pressure Injury (Bedsore)

NPUAP/EPUAP Staging System

Caused by sustained pressure and shear forces on bony prominences — sacrum, heels, hips. Pressure exceeding capillary pressure (~32 mmHg) cuts off blood supply, causing ischaemic necrosis. Common in ICU patients, stroke patients, and the elderly. Malnutrition is the single largest modifiable risk factor.

Post-Surgical Wound

Dehiscence, Infection, High-Risk Closures

Surgical wounds should heal within 2–3 weeks. When they don't — wound dehiscence (re-opening of a sutured wound) is one of the most feared post-operative complications. High-risk patients: malnourished, diabetic, elderly, obese, on corticosteroids, or post-major abdominal surgery. Pre-operative nutritional optimisation significantly reduces dehiscence rates.

Key Risk Indicators

Albumin <3.5 g/dL · Pre-albumin <15 mg/dL · BMI <18.5 or >35 · HbA1c >8% · Age >70 · Immunosuppression

Pre-op: 5–7 days Epithrive® Post-op: Start within 48 hrs

⚠️ Surgical wound dehiscence doubles hospital stay and multiplies cost. Arginine+Gln+HMB supplementation reduces post-surgical complications in malnourished patients (Barbul et al.)

🔥

Burns

Partial vs. Full Thickness

Burns create the most extreme nutritional demand of any wound type. The burn injury triggers hypermetabolism — metabolic rate rises 150–200% above normal. Massive protein catabolism occurs as the body tries to fuel the immune response and repair. Without aggressive nutritional support, burn patients deteriorate rapidly. Glutamine is particularly critical — it is the most depleted amino acid in burns.

Classification

Superficial (1st degree): Epidermis only — redness, no blisters. Heals spontaneously in 3–5 days.
Partial thickness (2nd degree): Epidermis + dermis. Blistering. Heals 10–21 days.
Full thickness (3rd degree): All skin layers destroyed. Requires grafting. Massive nutritional deficit.

Nutritional support: Day 1–2 post-burn 2nd–3rd degree: Critical indication
🦵

Venous Leg Ulcer (VLU)

Most common chronic wound globally

Caused by chronic venous insufficiency — failure of venous valves leads to blood pooling in the lower legs, fluid leakage into tissues (oedema), and progressive tissue hypoxia. The hypoxic, oedematous environment impairs all phases of wound healing. VLUs have a recurrence rate of 70%+ without correction of underlying venous disease. Nutrition supports healing even though the primary cause is mechanical.

Clinical Features

Location: Medial lower leg (gaiter area) · Shallow, irregular wound · Weeping, exudative · Surrounded by lipodermatosclerosis (firm, brown skin) · Typically painless unless infected

Primary Rx: Compression therapy Epithrive®: Nutritional adjunct
⏱️

Chronic Non-Healing Wound

Any wound stalled for 2+ weeks

A clinical designation — not a cause. Any wound that has failed to demonstrate measurable progress in 2+ weeks despite standard care is classified as chronic. The underlying cause may be any of the above, compounded by systemic factors. The defining biology is persistent inflammation with ECM destruction exceeding rebuilding. These patients often have multiple overlapping risk factors: malnutrition + diabetes + poor circulation + infection.

The SNAP Rule for Patient Identification
