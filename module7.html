Pre-Field Briefing

Three things to internalise before you walk into any doctor's office

Field success is 60% preparation and 40% execution. New MRs who struggle in the field almost always struggle because of preparation failure — not knowledge failure. You've studied six modules. This section distils everything into the three mental anchors that matter before every single call.

01

Know Your Single Objective

Before entering the room, write on your notepad: "Today my one objective is ________." It must be specific, achievable, and framed as a doctor action — not a sale. "Get him to try Epithrive® on his next stalled DFU patient" is an objective. "Sell Epithrive®" is a wish.

02

Know the First Question You'll Ask

Never open a call without a prepared clinical question. "How many DFU patients do you see per week who are stalling despite good dressings?" opens the conversation. "Good morning, I'm from Epithrive®" closes it. Prepare your opening question — not your opening pitch.

03

Know What You'll Leave Behind

Before walking in, decide exactly which one or two materials you'll leave. Doctor Summary only (first call). Doctor Summary + Case Study (second call). Full Formulation Explainer (for dietitian). Don't drop the entire bag. One targeted material creates more impact than five random ones.

The field mindset mantra: "I am not here to sell. I am here to understand a clinical problem and share a nutritional solution. If I understand their problem better than they expect me to, I earn the right to be heard. If I share evidence precisely and honestly, I earn the right to be trusted. If I ask for one small specific action, I earn the chance to come back."

The 3-Minute Detailing Drill

How to present the Doctor Summary A4 in exactly 180 seconds — practise this until it's automatic

Most doctors will give you 2–4 minutes on a first call. If you cannot deliver a credible, complete, compliant Epithrive® detailing in under 3 minutes — with the doctor summary in hand — you will lose the call before it begins. This drill gives you the sequence, the timing, and the exact language for each beat.

0:00–0:20

Context Setting — The Problem Statement

Set the clinical context before touching the product. 15–20 seconds. Makes everything that follows relevant.

"Doctor, in chronic wound patients — DFU, pressure injuries, post-surgical — the wound often stalls not because the local care is inadequate, but because the body lacks the systemic substrates to build new tissue."

Name the product, describe it precisely, position it as adjunct. 20 seconds.

Show the sachet visual"Adjunct" — not replacement

0:40–1:10

The Mechanism — 30 Seconds Max

One pathway, one mechanism, one clinical implication. Resist the urge to explain all 14 ingredients.

"Arginine is the rate-limiting substrate for nitric oxide — which drives angiogenesis and blood flow to the wound bed. In diabetic and malnourished patients, Arginine and Glutamine are systematically depleted. Epithrive® restores these specifically, which is why the clinical data shows a difference even when standard care is identical."

Primary finding: 72.4% mean wound area reduction in the Epithrive® arm versus 52.1% with standard care alone at Week 8 — p=0.004. Both arms received identical standard wound care. The Close — 40 Seconds

Leave with a specific, single, achievable action request. Then let there be silence.

"Doctor, would you consider trying Epithrive® on your next patient whose wound hasn't shown 30% improvement in 4 weeks, or whose albumin is below 3.5? Just one patient — so you can form your own clinical impression. I'll follow up in a week. [Hand over the A4 summary.] Thank you for your time."

The goal is 2:45–3:00 delivered naturally, not rushed. Practice daily for 5 days before your first live call. The sequence should feel conversational, not scripted.

Scored Role-Play Scenarios

6 complex field situations with full dialogue and evaluator rubrics — each scored on 5 dimensions

Scenario 1

Orthopaedic Surgeon — First call, dismissive, running late

Most common first-call situation — earn time in under 60 seconds or lose the visit

Medium ›
Setting Surgeon OPD. 3 patients waiting. The doctor looks up briefly when you enter and says nothing.
MR
Doctor, I'll keep this to 90 seconds. In post-debridement DFU patients — the wound often stalls because the systemic substrate supply doesn't match the metabolic demand. I work with Nuin Wellness on Epithrive® — a wound nutrition formula. Our Indian study: 72.4% vs 52.1% wound area reduction at Week 8, p=0.004. The PI was Dr. Bhanegaonkar, MS Ortho. May I leave a one-pager?
DR
These supplement companies come every week. Leave it with the receptionist. [turns away]
MR
Understood, Doctor. One thing that's different — this has an Indian clinical study, p=0.004, PI is an Orthopaedic Surgeon. May I ask — when a DFU patient stalls after good debridement, what do you currently do next? [pause] I'll leave the summary and call your receptionist for 5 minutes next week.
DR
Come on Thursday. I have more time in the afternoon.
Evaluator Rubric
Opening Hook 90%
Led with clinical problem not product name. Respected time constraint immediately.
Dismissal Handling 85%
Did not grovel or over-explain. Differentiated crisply ("Indian study, Ortho PI") and asked a question.
Evidence Delivery 80%
Key stat delivered precisely. Study ID could have been mentioned.
Close 95%
Secured a return appointment. Did not oversell. Clean exit.
Compliance 100%
No prohibited claims. Correct statistical framing throughout.
Overall Score: 90/100 — Pass

Excellent recovery from dismissal. The question at the end ("what do you do next?") was smart — it planted a clinical gap without demanding attention.

Coaching Notes
  • The moment the doctor dismisses you as "another supplement company" is the moment that defines the call. Do NOT apologise or try harder with more data — that confirms their assumption. Instead, differentiate crisply and ask a clinical question.
  • Securing "Thursday afternoon" is a win from a dismissal. Never leave without a next step.
  • The pause before "I'll leave the summary" is intentional — silence after a question gives the doctor space to answer. Don't fill it.
Scenario 2

Ward Nurse — Safe for diabetic patient with CKD Stage 3?

Clinical safety question from non-prescriber — must be accurate, not evasive

Moderate ›
Setting Ward nursing station. A Sister asks about a patient whose doctor has recommended Epithrive® — the patient is diabetic with CKD Stage 3 and is on metformin and a quinolone antibiotic.
SISTER
Doctor said to start Epithrive® for Mr. Patil — he's diabetic, CKD Stage 3, on ciprofloxacin. I want to make sure before I give it to him.
MR
Good thinking to check — let me give you the specific answers. CKD Stage 3 (eGFR 30–60): Epithrive® is safe at the standard 1 sachet per day dose. No dose reduction needed at Stage 3. The amino acid load is within acceptable limits. Monitor renal parameters if used long-term, as with any supplement in CKD, but Stage 3 is not a contraindication.
SISTER
And the ciprofloxacin?
MR
Important point — Zinc in Epithrive® can reduce ciprofloxacin absorption if taken simultaneously. The solution is simple: give Mr. Patil his ciprofloxacin first on an empty stomach — as is standard — wait 2–3 hours, then give Epithrive® dissolved in water. Morning antibiotic, midday or afternoon Epithrive®. Please note this spacing on his medication sheet so the family knows when they take over at home.
SISTER
And the diabetes — sugar in the product?
MR
No added sugar — this was a deliberate design decision because most wound patients are diabetic. Orange flavour is from natural flavouring. Safe with metformin and insulin. No blood glucose impact from the amino acid ingredients. He can take it safely.
Evaluator Rubric
Clinical Accuracy 100%
CKD Stage 3 answer correct. Drug interaction correctly identified and resolved with timing guidance.
Communication Clarity 95%
Answered each question in sequence without hedging. Practical, nurse-friendly language.
Safety First Attitude 100%
Validated the nurse's caution. Gave actionable guidance for the medication sheet — clinical partnership behavior.
Scope Discipline 90%
Stayed within MR knowledge scope. Could have flagged "check with doctor if any concern" for CKD monitoring.
Compliance 100%
No outcome claims made. Safety answers only, factual and precise.

This is precisely what MRs should be for nursing staff — a fast, reliable, clinically accurate resource. The medication sheet suggestion shows field IQ.

Coaching Notes
  • Never hedge on direct safety questions from nursing staff — they need clear, actionable answers, not "it depends." Give the specific answer, then add the caveat.
  • The drug interaction answer is critical to get right. Zinc + quinolone = space by 2–3 hours. This is a commonly encountered situation in wound patients on infection treatment.
  • Suggesting a note on the medication sheet earns trust immediately — it shows you're thinking about patient safety, not just product usage.
Scenario 3

Caregiver at pharmacy — "Does this actually work?"

Emotionally loaded, sceptical caregiver — requires empathy before evidence

Hard — Emotional ›
Setting Medical store near a surgical hospital. A son, roughly 35, is buying wound dressings for his diabetic mother. He has been told about Epithrive® by the pharmacist. He's spent 4 months managing her foot wound with no progress.
SON
I'm tired of spending money on things that don't work. Does this actually work or is it just marketing?
MR
I hear you — four months is exhausting. And I understand why trust is completely reasonable to withhold. I'm going to be honest with you: I can't promise you it will work for your mother. What I can tell you is what it is and what the evidence shows.
SON
Okay. Tell me.
MR
Everything you've used so far — dressings, medicines — has been working on the wound from the outside. What Epithrive® does is supply the building blocks the body needs internally: Arginine for blood flow to the wound, Glutamine to support the immune cells fighting infection, and Collagen precursors for new tissue. We did a clinical study — 60 patients, similar wound types. The Epithrive® group showed 72.4% wound reduction vs 52.1% with dressings alone at 8 weeks. That's a real difference — not dramatic marketing numbers.
SON
How much is it?
MR
₹3,995 for 10 sachets — about ₹400 per day. One sachet a day in water, orange flavour, no sugar — safe for your mother's diabetes. I'd suggest starting one box, and asking the doctor to look at the wound bed at the end of 10–12 days. If you see granulation tissue starting — pink, healthy tissue — that's the signal it's working. If there's no visible change at all, you can stop and you haven't lost much.
SON
I'll take one box. If nothing happens in 10 days, I'm done.
MR
That's completely fair. Let me give you this product information sheet — and my WhatsApp number. Message me at Day 10 and tell me what the wound looks like. I want to know too.
Evaluator Rubric
Empathy First 100%
Named the exhaustion, validated the scepticism before pitching. Zero defensiveness.
Honesty Calibration 100%
"I can't promise" and "not dramatic marketing numbers" built far more trust than a guarantee would have.
Evidence Framing 90%
Study stat landed cleanly. "Not dramatic marketing numbers" was a confident, trust-building phrase.
Low-Risk Trial Close 95%
One box, 10-day checkpoint, specific visual signal to watch for. Reduced financial and emotional risk perfectly.
Relationship Building 100%
"Message me at Day 10 — I want to know too." Extraordinary. Transforms a transaction into a partnership.
Overall Score: 97/100 — Exceptional

This is masterclass-level caregiver handling. The honesty, the low-risk trial offer, and the Day 10 follow-up ask all demonstrate field intelligence. The son will come back.

Coaching Notes
  • The worst thing you can do with an exhausted caregiver is start with clinical data. They don't care about the study until they trust you. Earn trust first — data second.
  • "I can't promise you it will work" is counterintuitively the most powerful sales statement in this scenario. It signals honesty, which is what this person needs more than hope.
  • The 10-day checkpoint with a visual signal ("pink granulation tissue") transforms a vague hope into a concrete test. Caregivers respond to specific, observable milestones.
  • Asking to be messaged at Day 10 turns you into a partner in the outcome — not a salesperson who moved on after the transaction.

Skeptical Diabetologist — "This is just another supplement company"

Category dismissal — must elevate Epithrive® beyond supplement perception in under 2 minutes

Medium-Hard ›
Setting Second call. Diabetologist who previously heard the India study but dismissed it. Returns today with a more pointed attitude.
DR
Look — I see 10 supplement companies a week. All of them have "studies." None of them actually change how I practice. Why are you different?
MR
That's a completely fair challenge — and I'd rather earn that answer than assert it. Let me be specific about what's different and let you judge. First: the formulation. Most wound supplements contain generic amino acids. Epithrive® delivers 7g L-Arginine — the specific precursor to nitric oxide that your DFU patients' endothelium can no longer produce adequately. That's not a supplement claim — that's a pathway your patients are missing.
DR
Go on.
MR
Second: the evidence behind this formulation class goes beyond one company study. Williams and Barbul published a double-blind RCT in Annals of Surgery showing significantly increased wound collagen deposition with Arginine+HMB+Glutamine in elderly volunteers. Tatti et al. showed DFU healing time dropped from 247 to 83 days. A 2021 meta-analysis of 44 human studies confirmed consistent effects. Our Indian study adds p=0.004 in 60 Indian patients — same direction as the global literature.

All right. What patient profile specifically?
MR
Your DFU patient who has a wound stalled beyond 4 weeks, albumin below 3.5 g/dL, or pre-albumin below 15 mg/dL — and is not making 30% progress despite standard care. That's the SNAP Rule we use. That patient has a measurable nutritional deficit driving the stall. Epithrive® addresses the deficit, not the wound directly.
DR
That's a reasonable case. Send me the references on WhatsApp and I'll review properly.

Mechanistic Depth 90%
Arginine-NO pathway in diabetic endothelium — exactly right for a diabetologist. Matched to specialty.

Annals of Surgery, Tatti, meta-analysis, India study — layered evidence cited naturally, not desperately.

Coaching Notes
  • When a doctor dismisses the category ("just another supplement"), the instinct is to over-defend. Resist it. Respond with specificity — mechanism, then evidence, then patient profile. Let precision do the work.

  • Having the SNAP Rule thresholds (albumin <3.5, pre-albumin <15, wound stalled 4+ weeks) ready to cite shows that you understand their clinical workflow — not just your product.

Coaching Notes
  • The third call with a positive outcome is your most important call. Don't just celebrate — immediately ask "how many more patients?" and "can we formalise this?"

  • Including the dietitian in the next meeting is not optional — it's the right clinical governance. Nutrition protocol changes require dietitian sign-off. Getting both in one room saves two calls.
Scenario 6

General Surgeon — "We tried it and it didn't work for one patient"

Negative feedback from a past trial — the most dangerous objection to handle poorly

Hard — Damage Control ›
Setting Return call on a General Surgeon who tried Epithrive® three weeks ago on a post-surgical patient. The patient had a severely infected wound that didn't heal despite Epithrive® + standard care. The doctor is now skeptical.
DR
We tried it. Didn't work. The wound didn't get better and frankly I think the family wasted money.
MR
Thank you for telling me directly — I really want to understand what happened. Can I ask — what was the wound status at the time Epithrive® was started? Was there active infection present?
DR
Yes — there was a deep surgical site infection. We were on IV antibiotics.
MR
That's really important context. In the presence of an active, deep surgical site infection — especially with IV antibiotic dependency — the wound biology is overwhelmed by infection. No nutritional supplement can substitute for infection control. Epithrive® is designed to support healing once the wound bed is stable: after infection is cleared and the wound is in the granulation or proliferative phase. In a raging infection, the substrate problem is secondary to the bacterial burden problem.
DR
So when should it be used?
MR
Ideally, in one of three windows: post-debridement once the wound is clean, once active infection is controlled (wound bed is red/pink, no purulence), or in a chronic stalled wound where infection is not the primary problem — nutritional deficit is. If the wound is red, granulating, or epithelialising but slow — that's the right patient. The patient you tried it on was in the wrong window. I should have been clearer about the patient selection criteria when I first presented. I'm sorry for that. Can we identify a patient in your current panel who is post-infection and in the granulation phase?
DR
Actually, yes — I have a patient who was infected last month. Infection is cleared now. Wound is clean but not closing.
MR
That's the ideal patient. Infection cleared, wound stable, not progressing — the stall is now nutritional. Would you try Epithrive® on this patient specifically?
Evaluator Rubric
Receiving Negative Feedback 100%
No defensiveness. Immediately asked a clarifying question. Turned feedback into investigation.
Root Cause Analysis 100%
Identified the correct reason for failure: active infection = wrong window. Clinically accurate and explained clearly.
Accountability 100%
"I should have been clearer — I'm sorry for that." This is the most important line in the scenario. Ownership without excuse.
Recovery Close 95%
Immediately pivoted to the right patient in the doctor's current panel. Specific, actionable, non-defensive.
Patient Selection Education 100%
Three clear windows defined. Doctor now knows exactly when to use Epithrive®. This prevents future wrong-window failures.
Overall Score: 99/100 — Masterclass

This is the hardest scenario — and this MR executed it perfectly. Accountability + clinical explanation + immediate pivot to right patient = a near-perfect damage-control call.

Coaching Notes
  • When a doctor says "we tried it and it didn't work" — never defend immediately. Ask what happened first. The answer usually reveals a patient selection problem, not a product failure.
  • "I should have been clearer about the patient selection criteria. I'm sorry for that." Taking ownership for the wrong-patient-selection is critical and counter-intuitive. It shifts the conversation from product failure to guidance failure — which is correctable.
  • The three windows (post-debridement clean, post-infection stable, chronic non-infectious stall) are the patient selection criteria the MR should have established before the trial. Always establish the right patient profile before a trial — not after a failure.
  • Getting from "it didn't work" to "would you try it on this patient" in one call is an exceptional outcome. Most MRs would lose the doctor permanently in this scenario.

Shadowing Framework

How to get the most from accompanied calls with your Area Manager

Every new MR must complete at least 5 accompanied field calls with their Area Manager before transitioning to solo calls. The shadowing program is not observation — it is structured practice with immediate feedback. Use these checklists to maximise every joint call.

📋 Pre-Call (Together)

10 minutes before entering the clinic

Discuss the specific objective for this call — one action to ask for

Agree who speaks — MR leads, AM observes (or AM demos, MR observes)

MR states the planned opening question out loud

MR identifies the primary evidence point to use

Materials confirmed and ready — correct leave-behind for this doctor

AM notes 2–3 specific behaviours to watch (opening, evidence delivery, close)

👁️ During the Call (AM Observes)

Note specific moments — not general impressions

Did MR open with clinical observation (not social)?

Did MR ask a needs-assessment question before pitching?

Did MR calibrate pitch to the doctor's answer?

Was evidence delivered with correct attribution (study name + stat + p-value)?

Were all claims compliant? No prohibited language?

Was the close specific and single? Did MR wait for the response?

Was the leave-behind handed over naturally — not dumped?

Did MR confirm the next step before leaving?

🗣️ Post-Call Debrief

Within 5 minutes of exiting — while memory is fresh

MR self-assesses first: "What went well? What would I change?"

AM gives one specific positive observation (exact moment, exact language)

AM gives one specific improvement area (with alternative language suggested)

Did the call achieve its stated objective? If not — why?

What is the follow-up action and who does it?

One thing to practice before the next call is identified

Debrief rule: MR self-assesses BEFORE AM gives feedback. This trains self-awareness — the single most important skill for long-term field development. An MR who can accurately diagnose their own call performance will improve 3× faster than one who waits for external feedback.

Common Field Mistakes

What new MRs typically get wrong in the first 30 days — and how to correct each one
❌ Pitching before listening

New MRs deliver the elevator pitch the moment they sit down — before asking a single question about the doctor's patients or challenges. The doctor hasn't connected the product to their problem yet, so nothing lands.

✓ Correction

Ask one needs-assessment question first. "How many DFU patients do you see per week who are stalling despite good dressings?" Then the pitch becomes an answer to a question they've just articulated.

❌ Citing the stat wrong under pressure

"72.4 percent" becomes "about 70 percent" or "nearly 75 percent" when the MR is nervous. Or "p=0.004" becomes "p less than 0.05." Imprecision signals insecurity — the opposite of clinical credibility.

✓ Correction

Drill the exact numbers until they're automatic. "72.4 versus 52.1, p equals 0.004." Write it on a card. Say it 20 times before your first call. Precision is what differentiates you from "just another MR."

❌ Leaving too much material

First call, nervous MR places the Doctor Summary, Formulation Explainer, Case Study, and clinical dossier on the desk. The doctor glances at the pile, doesn't touch any of it, and moves on.

✓ Correction

First call: Doctor Summary only. "I'm leaving just this one page — everything you need in 2 minutes of reading." The constraint creates value. "I'll bring the full formulation detail on Thursday" creates the next appointment.

❌ Vague close — "please recommend when appropriate"

This is the most common close failure. "When appropriate" gives the doctor no signal for when that is — so nothing changes. The MR leaves without a commitment and without a next step.

✓ Correction

"Your next DFU patient whose wound hasn't moved in 4 weeks and albumin is below 3.5 — would you try Epithrive® on that specific patient?" A patient profile trigger is a specific, recognisable moment — not a vague intention.

❌ Over-explaining under objection

Doctor says "the evidence isn't strong enough." MR responds with 5 studies, 8 statistics, and 3 meta-analyses — for 4 minutes. The doctor stops listening after 30 seconds. Desperation doesn't build credibility.

✓ Correction

Acknowledge → one clear response → stop. "You're right — the India study is a pilot. Behind it is a 2021 meta-analysis of 44 human studies and an Annals of Surgery RCT. Would you review the full CSR?" One bridge, one reference, one next step.

❌ Missing the follow-up window

The call goes well. The doctor seems interested. The MR sends a WhatsApp 4 days later — too late. The doctor has seen 60 more patients, forgotten the conversation, and the momentum is gone completely.

✓ Correction

WhatsApp within 24 hours — ideally the same evening. Reference the specific conversation: "As discussed today, the India study summary is attached." The specificity shows you were paying attention. The speed shows you're serious.

Handling Field Surprises

When the call doesn't go to script — how to stay calm and navigate
😤Another MR has given incorrect information about Epithrive® to the doctor›
Situation: The doctor says "I heard Epithrive® has serious kidney side effects from another representative who came last week."
Response: "I'm concerned to hear that — and I want to correct that directly. Epithrive® is safe in CKD Stage 1–3 at standard dosing. We recommend caution and dose monitoring in Stage 4, and nephrologist clearance in Stage 5. The amino acid load is within acceptable limits for the vast majority of wound patients. I'd like to understand where that information came from — it does not reflect our clinical data or the product's safety profile. May I send you our safety summary so you have the correct information in writing?"

Then: escalate immediately to your Area Manager. Incorrect information from any source — including another MR — is an urgent correction issue.
Never attack the other MR by name. Correct the information precisely. Offer written documentation. Escalate.
📵Doctor suddenly takes a 10-minute call mid-detailing›
Situation: You're at the evidence delivery stage — the best part of the call. The doctor's phone rings and they take a long call. When they return, they've completely lost the thread.
Response: Use the interruption as a reset opportunity. When they return, don't resume where you were — re-hook. "Doctor, while you were on that call I was thinking — the one data point from the India study that I think is most relevant for your DFU patients is this: 72.4% wound area reduction versus 52.1%, p=0.004. I'll leave this A4 summary. Could I book 5 minutes on your calendar for Thursday to go through the mechanism detail?"

You've compressed the rest of the call into the most important point and secured the next appointment — turning an interruption into an advance.
Interruptions are opportunities to re-prioritise. Drop everything else. Deliver the single most important point. Book the return.
❓Doctor asks a clinical question you genuinely don't know the answer to›
Situation: "Can this be used alongside methotrexate in a rheumatoid patient with a pressure ulcer?" You have no specific data on this interaction.
Response: "That's a specific clinical question I want to answer accurately rather than guess at. Methotrexate has its own nutritional interaction profile and I don't want to give you incorrect guidance. Let me confirm with our Medical Affairs team and come back to you by end of this week with the specific answer — in writing."

Then: actually do it. Message your Area Manager within 2 hours. Get the answer from Medical Affairs. Send it to the doctor by Thursday at the latest.
"I don't know — let me find out and come back" is a more trust-building answer than a confident wrong one. Doctors respect honesty. Follow-through is mandatory.
😡A hostile family member at the pharmacy demands a refund and says it didn't work›
Situation: A family member is angry, voice raised, saying they bought two boxes and the wound "got worse" — and they want their money back from the pharmacist.
Response: Step 1 — De-escalate first. "I can hear how frustrated you are — and I want to understand what happened. Can we step aside for a moment?" Step 2 — Gather information calmly: what type of wound, how long used, what changed. Step 3 — Acknowledge: "I'm sorry the experience wasn't what you hoped for." Step 4 — If the wound genuinely worsened: "Please have the doctor see the wound today — this needs clinical assessment, not just nutritional support." Step 5 — Escalate to Area Manager immediately. Do not discuss refund policy yourself — that is the company's domain.

Step 6 — Document the adverse feedback and escalate the same day.
An angry caregiver is a clinical signal, not just a complaint. Treat it as patient safety first, service issue second. Never argue. Always escalate adverse outcomes.
🏆A doctor asks you to join a ward round and present Epithrive® to the entire team›
Situation: An interested ICU physician says "Come back tomorrow at 9am — I want you to present Epithrive® to my team during morning rounds." This is an opportunity and a test.
Response: Accept immediately — then call your Area Manager the same evening. Prepare a 5-minute structured presentation: (1) The wound nutrition gap — 30 seconds; (2) Epithrive® formulation overview — 60 seconds; (3) India study headline data — 60 seconds; (4) Patient selection criteria (SNAP Rule) — 60 seconds; (5) Dosing, safety summary, and how to order — 60 seconds.

Bring printed Doctor Summaries for every person in the room. Stand, speak clearly, and handle questions precisely. Use only compliant language — a room full of clinicians will catch any overclaim. End with: "Happy to answer any questions now or via WhatsApp individually."
Ward round presentations are the highest-value single activity in hospital MR sales. One successful ward round can create 5–10 simultaneous recommenders. Prepare the night before. Never improvise at this level.
