The Medical Sales Mindset

The difference between an MR who is tolerated and one who is trusted

The most effective medical representatives are not salespeople — they are clinical advisors who happen to represent a product. Every technique in this module is built on that foundation. Doctors see dozens of MRs every week. The ones they remember, respect, and act on are the ones who come with knowledge, not just a catalogue.

🎓

Clinical Advisor

You know wound biology (Module 1), wound care (Module 2), formulation science (Module 3), and evidence (Module 4). Lead with this. A doctor who sees you as knowledgeable will listen to what you say next.

🔑

Solve Their Problem

You are not here to sell Epithrive®. You are here to solve the problem of wound patients who aren't healing despite good local care. The product is the solution — but the problem is the conversation starter.

🤝

Long-term Relationship

One recommendation from a trusted doctor generates 5–15 patients. One engaged doctor who writes "Epithrive® for wound nutrition support" on every applicable discharge slip is worth more than 50 one-time interactions.

The conversion mindset shift: Stop thinking "how do I close this doctor today?" Start thinking "how do I earn the right to be trusted as their wound nutrition resource?" Trust leads to recommendation. Recommendation leads to patient outcomes. Patient outcomes lead to more recommendations. The call is the beginning — not the transaction.

The PRECBAC Call Structure

7 steps — from pre-call planning to post-call follow-up. Master this sequence cold.

Every high-performing doctor call follows a structure — whether the MR knows it or not. PRECBAC makes that structure explicit and repeatable. A 15-minute doctor call, executed well using this framework, is more effective than an hour of unstructured conversation.

P
Pre-Call
⏱ 10–15 min before the call

Pre-Call Planning

Research the doctor: specialty, patient profile, OPD days, previous interactions, what leave-behinds have been shared. Define your objective for this specific call — not "to sell" but a specific, concrete outcome.

Define your call objective: "Today I want to introduce the India study to Dr. Sharma and leave the doctor summary. I want him to try Epithrive® on his next DFU patient with albumin <3.5."
R
Rapport
⏱ 30–60 seconds

Rapport Opening — Clinical, Not Social

Do not open with "Good morning Doctor, how are you?" — this is what every other MR says. Open with a clinical observation or question relevant to their specialty. Shows you did your homework. Earns their attention in the first 10 seconds.

Opening for an Orthopaedic Surgeon: "Doctor, I've been seeing a lot of cases where the wound stalls after debridement — clean bed but no granulation. I wanted to understand what you're seeing in your post-debridement DFU patients before I share something."
E
Explore
⏱ 2–3 minutes

Explore — Needs Assessment Before Pitching

Ask before you tell. The doctor's answer will tell you which angle to take. Never pitch before you understand their specific pain. One well-placed question is worth ten minutes of monologue.

Power questions to ask:
"How many DFU / pressure ulcer patients do you see per week who are taking longer than expected to heal?"
"When a wound stalls despite good dressings, what's your current next step?"
"Do you routinely check albumin or do a nutritional screen on your wound patients?"
C
Connect
⏱ 1–2 minutes

Connect — Bridge from Their Problem to Your Solution

Link what the doctor just told you directly to what Epithrive® addresses. This is where your Modules 1–3 knowledge earns its pay. The bridge should feel logical, not salesy — a natural consequence of the problem they described.

Bridge: "So you're seeing 3–4 DFU patients per week where the wound has stalled despite standard care. That's exactly the scenario where the systemic nutritional substrate is the limiting factor — not the local wound management. What the body needs to form granulation tissue — Arginine, Glutamine, Collagen precursors — is typically depleted in your diabetic, elderly patients. That's precisely what Epithrive® was formulated to restore."
B
Build
⏱ 3–5 minutes

Build — Evidence Delivery (Calibrated to This Doctor)

Present only the 1–2 most relevant evidence points for this doctor's specialty and concern. Do not dump all the evidence at once — select and calibrate. Leave the rest for follow-up calls.

For a surgeon on their first call: Lead with the India study (72.4% vs 52.1%, p=0.004). Name the PI (Dr. Bhanegaonkar, MS Ortho). Leave the A4 summary.

For a skeptical diabetologist: Lead with Tatti 2012 (DFU healing time 247→83 days). Follow with the Arginine-NO pathway in diabetic endothelium.

For a dietitian: Lead with ESPEN/EPUAP guidelines recommending Arg+Zn+VitC. Then show how Epithrive® exceeds those recommendations by adding Glutamine, HMB, and Marine Collagen.
A
Ask
⏱ 30–60 seconds

Ask for Action — Specific, Small, Achievable

Never leave without asking for something specific. Not "please recommend Epithrive®" — that's too abstract. Ask for the smallest concrete action that moves the relationship forward. Small commitments lead to larger ones.

First call closes:
"Doctor, could you try Epithrive® on your next DFU patient whose albumin is below 3.5 or whose wound hasn't shown 30% reduction in 4 weeks? Just one patient — I'd love to share the outcome."

Second call close: "Could I ask you to write 'wound nutrition support — Epithrive®' on the discharge slip for any patient with a stalled wound this month?"
C
Continue
⏱ Within 24–48 hours post-call

Continue — Follow-Up That Earns the Next Conversation

The call ends in the clinic. The relationship continues on WhatsApp. A timely, relevant follow-up message that references something specific from the conversation is what separates an MR who gets a second meeting from one who doesn't.

WhatsApp follow-up template:
"Dr. Sharma, thank you for your time today. As discussed — the India study comparing Epithrive® + standard care vs standard care alone is attached. Key finding: 72.4% vs 52.1% wound area reduction at Week 8 (p=0.004). Would be glad to discuss further. [Attach: Doctor Summary PDF]
Disclaimer: Dietary supplement. Not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease."

Objection Handling

20+ objections across 6 categories — understand the real concern behind each word
The objection mindset: An objection is not a rejection — it is a question in disguise. When a doctor says "it's too expensive," they're asking "is this worth it?" When they say "there's no evidence," they're asking "how do I know this works?" Your job is to hear the question behind the words and answer that.
💰 Category A — Price & Value Objections
Price "My patients can't afford ₹3,995." ›
What They Really Mean

The price sounds high compared to a box of dressings. They don't yet see the total cost picture or the value relative to the problem.

Your Response Strategy

Reframe from cost to cost-per-day and cost vs. outcome. Then offer the trial pack as a lower-risk entry point.

"Doctor, ₹3,995 for a 10-sachet box is ₹400 per day — that's less than a single dressing change in most wound care protocols. Your patient is already spending ₹500–1,000 per day on dressings and clinic visits. A case we documented had a patient who spent ₹3.5 lakh before starting Epithrive®. The 25-day course cost less than 2% of that total. Faster healing means fewer dressing changes, fewer clinic visits, and lower overall cost. For families dealing with a chronic wound, ₹400/day for faster recovery is not expensive — it's an investment with a measurable return."
Offer a trial pack for first-time patients. "Let's start with a trial — if the family sees early progress, the commitment follows."
Price "Insurance won't cover a nutraceutical. My patient has to pay out of pocket." ›
The Real Concern

Doctor is concerned about patient affordability and the friction of recommending something the patient has to self-pay for.

Response Strategy

Validate — then flip to cost-outcome. The doctor's patients are already paying out of pocket for dressings, consultations, and antibiotics.

"You're right — and that's true for most dressings and many antibiotics beyond the basic formulary too. The question is whether the out-of-pocket cost delivers value. ₹400/day for Epithrive® vs. ₹3,000–8,000/day in hospitalisation costs — if it shortens the wound journey by even 2 weeks, the family has saved significantly. The India study showed 39% better healing at Week 8. That translates into fewer clinic visits, fewer dressings, and potentially avoiding a surgical re-intervention."
Propose: "For your admitted patients — consider recommending it as part of the discharge plan. Families at discharge are motivated to invest in faster recovery."
Price "What's the cost per month? The family is already spending a lot." ›
The Real Concern

Family budget fatigue — chronic wound patients have often been spending heavily for months. Concern about adding another recurring cost.

Response Strategy

Reframe as a time-limited investment, not an indefinite recurring cost. Epithrive® is used until wound closure — not forever.

"The good news is this is not a lifetime supplement — it's used until wound closure. For a moderate wound, that's typically 4–6 weeks: 4–5 boxes maximum, ₹16,000–20,000 total. Compare that to the ₹3.5 lakh this family may have already spent on hospitalisation, surgery, and dressings before starting targeted nutrition. The question is not 'can they afford it?' — it's 'can they afford the wound staying open for another two months?'"
Anchor to wound duration: "If we can accelerate closure by 3–4 weeks, what does that save them in dressings and clinic visits?"
🔬 Category B — Evidence & Data Objections
Evidence "There's no randomised controlled trial for Epithrive® specifically." ›
The Real Concern

High evidentiary standard. Doctor is distinguishing between formulation-class evidence and product-specific RCT evidence. This is a legitimate and sophisticated objection.

Response Strategy

Acknowledge the point precisely. Then explain the two-level evidence structure: product-level (India study) + formulation class (global RCTs). This is how all clinical nutrition products launch.

"That's a fair and precise distinction — and you're right. Epithrive® has a prospective Indian comparative study (n=60, p=0.004), not an RCT. The RCT evidence is at the formulation-class level: Williams and Barbul (2002, Annals of Surgery) — double-blind RCT, collagen deposition in elderly volunteers; Cereda et al. — RCT, 2× faster pressure ulcer healing vs standard ONS. A 2021 meta-analysis of 44 human studies confirmed significant effects. Every new clinical nutrition product follows this path — formulation-class evidence first, product-specific RCT as the evidence base matures. We're actively working toward publication of the India study. Would you consider a trial in selected patients while that process continues?"
Offer the full CSR document. "The complete clinical study report is available if you'd like to review the methodology in detail."
Evidence "The Armstrong 2014 RCT on this formulation class showed no significant difference." ›
The Real Concern

Doctor has read the literature carefully. This shows engagement — not hostility. Treat this as an opportunity to demonstrate clinical depth.

Response Strategy

Acknowledge the study correctly. The Armstrong primary endpoint was negative — but subgroup analysis showed benefit specifically in malnourished patients. That subgroup IS Epithrive®'s target.

"You're right to raise Armstrong — and it's exactly the right study to understand carefully. The primary outcome (wound closure in the overall population at 16 weeks) showed no significant difference. But the subgroup analysis told a different story: patients with pre-albumin ≤17 mg/dL and poor limb perfusion — the nutritionally compromised group — showed meaningful benefit. That is precisely our target patient: malnourished, slow-healing wound with stalled biology. The lesson from Armstrong is that whole-population effects are diluted when you include patients who don't have a nutritional deficit. Our India study deliberately focused on wound patients — and showed p=0.004."
Follow-up: "For your patients with albumin under 3.5 — that's the Armstrong-proven subgroup. That's where Epithrive® is most appropriate."
Evidence "n=60 is too small. I need larger data." ›
The Real Concern

Statistical power concern. Valid — smaller studies can be underpowered, producing spurious findings.

Response Strategy

Validate, then redirect to the global body of evidence which is far larger — and consistent in direction with the India study.

"You're right that n=60 is a pilot-scale study — and that's exactly how we position it: an Indian pilot generating localised data for an already well-evidenced formulation class. Behind the India study is a meta-analysis of 44 human studies (Arribaslopez 2021), multiple RCTs by Barbul, Cereda, and Oesser, and specific DFU data from Tatti et al. — all consistent with our India finding. A p=0.004 in a 60-patient study is actually a strong signal — it means the effect size is large enough to be clearly visible in a small sample."
Offer: "I can share the meta-analysis reference if that's useful alongside the India study."

🔧 Category E — Practical & Clinical Objections
Practical "My patient is on dialysis / has CKD. Can they take this?" ›
The Real Concern

Safety concern — CKD patients have restricted amino acid intake due to renal handling of nitrogen. Legitimate clinical concern.

Response Strategy

"The answer depends on CKD stage. Stage 1–3 (eGFR >30): safe at standard dosing. Stage 4: use 1 sachet/day with monitoring of serum urea and creatinine. Stage 5/dialysis: not recommended without nephrology clearance — the 14g combined amino acid load needs careful management in ESRD. Interestingly, Sipahi et al. (BMC Nephrology 2013) used this formulation class specifically in diabetic haemodialysis patients — 63.6% showed wound depth improvement in 4 weeks. If your patient is on dialysis, loop in the nephrologist and frame it as: 'wound healing is impaired without these substrates — can we trial it with close monitoring?'"
Offer the Sipahi reference: "I can share the BMC Nephrology paper — it's the most relevant evidence for dialysis patients with wounds."
Practical "My patient is on antibiotics for wound infection. Can they take it simultaneously?" ›
The Real Concern

Drug-nutrient interaction concern. Specifically about Zinc and tetracycline/quinolone absorption.

Response Strategy

Yes — but with a timing adjustment for specific antibiotic classes. Simple 2–3 hour separation solves the interaction completely.

"Yes, absolutely — and this is important for active wound infections, where Glutamine is most needed for immune support. One timing consideration: Zinc in Epithrive® can reduce absorption of tetracyclines and quinolones (like ciprofloxacin) if taken simultaneously. The solution is simple: take the antibiotic first on an empty stomach, wait 2–3 hours, then take Epithrive®. In practice, antibiotics are typically morning/evening — Epithrive® can be taken midday or at a different time. The wound healing benefit of maintaining Epithrive® through antibiotic treatment far outweighs the minor scheduling adjustment."
Practical tip: "Suggest the family note on the sachet — morning antibiotic, afternoon Epithrive®. Simple, no interaction."
Practical "My patients are elderly and don't comply with supplements. They'll stop taking it." ›

No taste of medicine. For elderly patients who resist supplements, the caregiver can mix it into water or a cold drink — they often don't even notice they're taking it. The key insight is that the person administering Epithrive® is the caregiver — and caregivers of wound patients are highly motivated. They've watched their family member suffer with a non-healing wound. One sachet per day is not a compliance challenge when the caregiver understands what it's doing."
